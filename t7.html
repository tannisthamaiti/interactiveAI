Finding the Extremes: max() and min() üìàüìâ

Imagine you have a list of high scores from a game. You want to know the absolute best score and the lowest score. NumPy makes this a breeze!

  • .max(): Finds the largest value in your array.
  • .min(): Finds the smallest value in your array.

Example Code:

import numpy as np
game_scores = np.array([120, 85, 205, 150, 99])
print("Highest Score:", game_scores.max()) # Output: 205
print("Lowest Score:", game_scores.min())  # Output: 85

Calculating the Average: mean() üìä

The **mean** is the "typical" value. You add all the numbers and divide by how many there are. NumPy's mean() function does this for you instantly!

The formula for the mean ($\mu$) is: $$ \mu = \frac{\sum x_i}{N} $$

Example Code:

import numpy as np
student_heights = np.array([150, 155, 160, 148, 152]) # Heights in cm
print("Average Height:", student_heights.mean()) # Output: 153.0

Finding the Middle: median() üìè

The **median** is the number that's right in the middle after you sort the data. It's great for ignoring super high or super low values that might throw off the average.

To find it: 1. Sort the numbers. 2. Pick the one in the middle. (If there's an even number of items, you average the two middle ones).

Example Code:

import numpy as np
ages = np.array([13, 12, 15, 14, 25]) # Sorted: [12, 13, 14, 15, 25] -> Middle is 14
print("Median age:", np.median(ages)) # Output: 14.0

The Most Popular: mode() üëë

The **mode** is the value that appears **most often** in your data. Think of it as the most popular or most frequent item.

Important: NumPy doesn't have its own mode function! We borrow it from another famous math library called SciPy.

Example Code:

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats # We import the stats part of SciPy

favorite_numbers = np.array([7, 4, 1, 7, 8, 7, 4])
mode_result = stats.mode(favorite_numbers)
print(mode_result) # Output: ModeResult(mode=7, count=3)

Measuring Spread: std() ‚ÜîÔ∏è

The **Standard Deviation** (std) is a measure of how spread out your numbers are from the average (mean).

  • A low std means the numbers are all close together and consistent.
  • A high std means the numbers are all over the place and vary a lot.

The formula for standard deviation ($\sigma$) looks complex, but it just finds the average distance from the mean: $$ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(x_i - \mu)^2}{N}} $$

Example Code:

import numpy as np
# Player A is very consistent
player_a_scores = np.array([10, 11, 10, 12, 10]) 
# Player B is all over the place
player_b_scores = np.array([5, 1, 20, 15, 2]) 

print("Player A std:", player_a_scores.std()) # Output: ~0.89
print("Player B std:", player_b_scores.std()) # Output: ~7.45
