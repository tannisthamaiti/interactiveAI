The Dawn of AI

From a Simple Question to the First "Thinking" Machines

The Big Question

In 1950, a brilliant mathematician named Alan Turing asked something that started it all: "Can machines think?"

He didn't want a simple yes or no. He wanted a way to actually TEST it!

What do you think is harder for a computer to do?

Solve 1,000 math problems in 1 second. Have a normal 5-minute chat about the weather.

The Imitation Game

Turing's idea was a test, now called the Turing Test. A human judge sends messages to both a human and a computer. If the judge can't tell which is the machine, the machine passes!

Conversation A
I like pizza.
Pizza is a dish of Italian origin.
Conversation B
I like pizza.
Oh nice! What's your favorite topping?

Which conversation is more likely to be with a human?

A B

1956: The Birth of "AI"

Turing's idea inspired a group of scientists. In 1956, they met at a special workshop at Dartmouth College.

It was here that computer scientist John McCarthy came up with the name for this new field: "Artificial Intelligence".

What do you think their main goal was?

To make machines learn and solve problems like humans. To build faster calculators.

The First "Thinking" Program

Right around the same time, the Logic Theorist (1956) was created. It's often called the first AI program.

It wasn't chatting about pizza, but it did something amazing: it proved mathematical theorems, and even found a more clever proof for one than a human had!

If you were creating the first AI, what problem would you make it solve?

Do my homework Play chess Tell me a new joke every day

From an Idea to a Program

So, in just a few years, AI went from a philosophical question to a real field of study with its first working programs!
